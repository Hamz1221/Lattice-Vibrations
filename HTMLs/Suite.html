Given a piece of solid, how can we model sound waves moving through it?

Let us assume that this is a simple solid, made of identical atoms spaced evenly. We know that atoms in a solid lattice is held together by binding forces, e.g. ionic, covalent or metallic. Think about the Lennard-Jones potential.

Let us model the binding forces using springs, i.e. all atoms are connected to their nearest neighbours by identical springs. This works in the small displacement limit, or at a temperature lower than melting point.

How does this model look like?

{{sectionTitleLong[1]}}

We start with the one dimensional case: imagine having an infinitely long chain of identical atoms. Now approximate their only interactions are only through the identical springs connected to their nearest neighbours. The most general solution is: $$ u_n(t) = Re \sum_{k}\tilde{u}_k e^{i(nka-\omega_kt)} $$ where $u_n$ is the displacement of the $n^{th}$ atom and $k$ is the wavevector of the incoming wave.

Here we simulate for a single $k$: $$ u_n(t) = u_k \cos(nka-\omega_kt)$$ For any given $k$: $$ \omega_k = 2 \omega_D \left| \sin \left( \frac{k a}{2} \right) \right|$$ where $$ \omega_D = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}}$$ $\kappa$ is the spring constant, $m$ is the mass of a single atom, $a$ is the atomic spacing and $\omega_D$ is also known as the Debye frequency. Here, we assume $a = \omega_D = 1$ and $\tilde{u}_k$ has zero phase for simplicity.

$u_k$ represents the responds of the $n^{th}$ atom to wave $k$. Notice that when $r \to \pm 1$, there are two standing waves. The alternating atoms move in phase. (Reduce $u_k$ to see this effect better.)

{{sectionTitleLong[2]}}

Now consider an infinitely wide sheet of identical atoms. The most general solution is: $$ \textbf{u}_n(t) = Re \sum_{\textbf{k}}\tilde{\textbf{u}}_\textbf{k} e^{i(\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{r} - \omega_\textbf{k} t)} $$ where $\textbf{u}_n$ is the displacement of the $n^{th}$ atom and $\textbf{k}$ is the wavevector of the incoming wave.

We simulate for a single $\mathbf{k}$: $$ \textbf{u}_n(t) = \textbf{u}_\textbf{k} \cos(\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{r}-\omega_k t)$$ For any given $\mathbf{k}$: $$ \omega^2_{\mathbf{k}} = 4 \omega^2_D \left[ \sin^2 \left(\frac{k_x a}{2} \right) + \sin^2 \left(\frac{k_y a}{2} \right) \right]$$ where $$ \omega_D = \sqrt{\frac{\kappa}{m}}$$ $\kappa$ is the spring constant, $m$ is the mass of a single atom, $a$ is the atomic spacing and $\omega_D$ is also known as the Debye frequency. We also assume $a = \omega_D = 1$ and $\tilde{\textbf{u}}_\textbf{k}$ has zero phase for simplicity.

We also trace the wave, which travels at phase velocity $\mathbf{v}=\frac{\mathbf{k}}{w}$.

$\textbf{u}_k$ represents the responds of the $n^{th}$ atom to wave $\textbf{k}$. Notice that $|\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{u}_k|$ and $|\textbf{k} \times \textbf{u}_k|$ determine the type of wave: longitudinal, transverse or a combination of both. Try making $|\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{u}_k| = 0 $ or $|\textbf{k} \times \textbf{u}_k| = 0 $. What are the types of wave you see in these respective cases?

{{sectionTitleLong[3]}}

Finally, we look at a three dimensional infinite crystal lattice. This simulation is meant for completeness. If this looks confusing for you, you may wish to return to the two dimensional case as it encompasses all the essentials of physics involved.

Note that you may experience difficulties in rotating the camera - try 'orbital rotation' found on the top right corner of the plot.

A reminder of the general solution: $$ \textbf{u}_n(t) = Re \sum_{\textbf{k}}\tilde{\textbf{u}}_\textbf{k} e^{i(\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{r} - \omega_\textbf{k} t)} $$ For a single $\mathbf{k}$: $$ \textbf{u}_n(t) = \textbf{u}_k \cos(\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{r}-\omega_k t)$$ For any given $\mathbf{k}$: $$ \omega^2_{\mathbf{k}} = $$ $$ 4 \omega^2_D \left[ \sin^2 \left(\frac{k_x a}{2} \right) + \sin^2 \left(\frac{k_y a}{2} \right) + \sin^2 \left(\frac{k_z a}{2} \right) \right]$$ where all the symbols have the same meanings as before.

The types of waves available are still longitudinal, transverse or a combination of both. This is indicated by $|\textbf{k} \cdot \textbf{u}_k|$ and $|\textbf{k} \times \textbf{u}_k|$.
